# LandPlan — Competitive Market Analysis

*Who’s in the market, and why the property owner quadrant is wide open*

**landplan.app · April 2026 · Internal / Stakeholder Use**

-----

## Executive Summary

LandPlan operates at the intersection of GIS mapping and long-horizon property planning for individual landowners — a combination that no existing product serves well. The competitive landscape divides cleanly into four buckets, none of which occupy LandPlan’s target position:

- **Land id® / MapRight** — strong parcel and property data browser for real estate professionals and land buyers; minimal planning, no owner-vision features
- **Aspire PropertyIntel** — precision measurement and takeoff tool for landscape contractors bidding commercial work; $199–$499/mo pricing reflects a B2B audience
- **STACK CT** — construction estimating and takeoff platform for contractors; no map-centric visualization for property owners
- **onX Hunt** — affordable land navigation for hunters; strong on public/private boundary data, zero planning capability

The property owner who wants to stand on their raw land and build a 5-to-15-year vision for it — with maps, 3D visualization, projects, and contractor quoting — has no purpose-built tool today. LandPlan’s positioning in that quadrant is unchallenged.

The most immediate pricing reference point is Land id® Pro (~$50/mo), which is the closest audience overlap (individual landowners). A LandPlan consumer tier in the $15–$30/mo range would undercut that meaningfully while delivering a dramatically richer planning capability.

## Competitor Profiles

### 1. Land id® (formerly MapRight)

Land id® is the leading property identity and interactive mapping platform for real estate professionals and landowners. Originally launched as MapRight in 2014, it rebranded in 2023 with an expanded focus on individual consumers via a new “Discover” tier.

**What it does well**

- Nationwide parcel data and ownership details for 150M+ properties
- Rich GIS layers: soil, floodplains, contours, public lands, ownership boundaries
- Mobile app (iOS and Android) with offline access
- Map sharing by embed, link, or email — strong for real estate listings
- 3D terrain view for exploring property topography

**What it doesn’t do**

- No project planning, sequencing, or activity tracking
- No 3D model placement or shadow simulation
- No stakeholder or contractor collaboration for improvement projects
- No long-horizon vision or mission statement for a property
- No data sovereignty — user data lives on Land id’s servers, not the owner’s Drive

**Who it serves**
Primarily real estate brokers, appraisers, and serious land buyers conducting due diligence. The Discover tier extends reach to landowners and the “land curious,” but the product’s DNA is research and listing, not stewardship.

-----

### 2. Aspire PropertyIntel

PropertyIntel is the next generation of Go iLawn and LandOne, now integrated into the Aspire landscape business management platform. It is a measurement and estimating tool built for landscape and irrigation contractors.

**What it does well**

- Precision takeoff measurements over aerial imagery
- Automated time and material estimating from contractor pricing databases
- Visual proposals with color-coded project sitemaps
- Aspire CRM and business management integration
- AI-assisted slope visualization (ProMaps)
- Done-for-you measurement service (PropertyIntel Complete)

**What it doesn’t do**

- Not designed for property owners — no owner-side vision, projects, or planning
- No owner-facing collaboration or stakeholder management
- No 3D models, shadow simulation, or long-horizon timeline
- Pricing ($199–$499+/mo) positioned for businesses, not individual landowners

**Who it serves**
Landscape and irrigation contractors using it to bid, win, and service commercial jobs. The property owner is the customer’s customer here — PropertyIntel serves the contractor, not the landowner directly.

-----

### 3. STACK CT (Takeoffs and Estimating)

STACK is a cloud-based construction takeoff and estimating platform used by general contractors, specialty contractors, and subcontractors. At $250/mo for a basic plan, it serves the professional construction trade.

**What it does well**

- Digital plan and blueprint uploads with measurement layering
- Material quantity calculations and cost estimates
- Activity and bid management with dependency tracking
- Contractor collaboration and proposal generation

**What it doesn’t do**

- No GIS mapping — works from uploaded PDF plans, not live aerial imagery
- No property owner features — designed for the contractor bidding the work
- No 3D visualization, shadow simulation, or long-term property vision
- No stakeholder or family-sharing model

**Who it serves**
Construction companies and specialty trades estimating projects from architect drawings. The tool assumes you’re the builder or GC, not the landowner commissioning the work.

-----

### 4. onX Hunt

onX Hunt is a GPS navigation and land ownership mapping app built for hunters. At $30–$100/yr, it is the most consumer-priced option reviewed here and has strong brand recognition among outdoors enthusiasts.

**What it does well**

- Public/private land boundary overlays across all 50 states
- Offline GPS navigation and waypoint marking
- Land ownership identification and adjacency data
- Weather integration and terrain visualization
- Very affordable annual pricing

**What it doesn’t do**

- No project planning, improvement sequencing, or timelines
- No photo documentation tied to map features
- No 3D models or shadow simulation
- No collaboration for family members or contractors
- No storage integration — map data stays in the onX ecosystem

**Who it serves**
Hunters and outdoor recreationists navigating public and private land. A small subset of users are landowners using it to understand their own property boundaries, but the tool offers nothing for what to build or how to sequence improvements.

## Strategic Implications

### The gap is real — and it’s structural

The tools that exist either serve contractors (who measure and estimate land for profit) or real estate professionals (who research and sell land). Nobody is building for the person who owns the land and wants to steward it over decades. That structural gap exists because consumer land tools historically lacked monetization clarity — LandPlan’s SaaS model, Google Drive integration, and planning depth give it a path that pure-mapping apps don’t have.

### Pricing has room to breathe

The cheapest tool with any meaningful mapping overlap is Land id® at ~$7–15/mo for basic tiers or ~$50/mo for Pro. LandPlan can position at $15–30/mo and deliver far more planning value than Land id® at any tier, while being dramatically less expensive than the contractor tools ($199–$499/mo) that landowners might otherwise try to repurpose.

### The closest risk: Land id® expanding upmarket

Land id® has the strongest brand and widest reach among individual landowners today. If they added project planning and stakeholder collaboration, they could compete directly. Their current trajectory — real estate professional tooling, AI-assisted research — suggests their expansion focus is elsewhere, but it’s worth monitoring.

### The contractor quoting module is a genuine moat

No consumer land tool offers a structured contractor quoting workflow. LandPlan’s Quote Packages — scoped visibility, magic links, side-by-side bid comparison — give a landowner capabilities that previously required hiring a GC or architect to coordinate. This feature differentiates LandPlan not just from mapping tools but from general project management tools like Notion or Asana that some owners might cobble together.

### Data ownership is an underrated differentiator

Every competitor stores user data in their own infrastructure. LandPlan’s architecture — all files on the owner’s Google Drive — is a meaningful trust differentiator, particularly for landowners sharing sensitive property information with family members and contractors. This is worth making explicit in marketing.

### Mobile is the next frontier

onX Hunt’s success ($30–100/yr, millions of users) demonstrates that consumers will pay for good mobile land tools. LandPlan’s planned offline-first mobile app — with BLE GPS and field measurement — could take the same audience and give them something onX never could: a connection back to their property’s plan.
